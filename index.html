Light Sensor

April 2019

I and another student built a light meter as our final project for the Electronic Circuits and Systems class. This device has four main components: Wheatstone bridge, difference amplifier, noise filter, and the Arduino control board. Each component was constructed and tested separately to make sure they operated properly before assembling the final circuit. Using a flashlight, the circuit was tested to see if the screen displayed a ‘0’ under ambient luminosity and the screen displayed a ‘9’ under maximum light intensity.

  • Partner: Balram Kandoria.
  • Category: Electronics.

Truss Bridge

April 2018

The bridge broke under 12.6 pound of force.

The structure didn't collapse completely when one stringer and several floor beams broke.

The bridge weights 0.21 oz and can carry a load of 940 times of its weight.

This bridge was my mid-term design project for the Statics class. The materials for this project were limited to wood-glue and 1/8 inch balsa wood strips. The bridge was also required to withstand a minimum static load of 350 times of its weight. My bridge was awarded extra credit for being the lightest bridge in the class.

  • Category: Structure.

Wing

November 2018 - May 2019

The airfoils were cut by a laser cutter.

The pieces of the wing were connected together using epoxy.

The final assembly of the 8-ft wide wing.

In this project, I worked in a team of five engineering students to build a rectangular high wing for the aircraft of AMAT (Advanced Modeling Aeronautics Team) at UC Davis. The wing was made of two basswood bars reinforced by carbon fiber and coated with plastic film. The airfoils were made of balsa wood and precisely cut by laser cutter. Although the team was very successful with our design report and presentation, in the competition our aircraft could not complete the flying mission due to several technical issues with the roll control system.

  • Partners: Linda Wu, Zoren Habana, Heather Lynch, William Moore.
  • Category: Aeronautical-structure.

CPU Cooling

May 2019

The processors in electronic devices process data based on the movement of electronic impulses. When the temperature of the processor rises, the computing speed becomes slower. Improper heat dissipation can eventually make the chip crash and burn. With Moore's Law predicting that processor's speed for computers will double about every 18 months, proper cooling has become essential in the electronics industry. In this project, I will examine how heat sink dissipates heat from the processor to keep the devices operating properly. ( View full analysis )
